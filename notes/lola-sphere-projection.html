Mathematical Geophysics

Projecting LOLA Data onto a Sphere

A self-contained derivation of the geometry, coordinate maps, Jacobians, and discrete approximations needed to take NASA LOLA lunar raster data and render it as a three-dimensional Moon.

This note explains the mathematics behind the pipeline \[ \text{LOLA raster} \longrightarrow \text{radius field on } S^2 \longrightarrow \text{embedded lunar surface in } \mathbb{R}^3. \] The goal is not merely to say how to display the Moon in software, but to make precise what object is being represented, what approximations are introduced by rasterization and resampling, and why the standard graphics construction is mathematically legitimate.

The central idea is simple: a global lunar DEM is a scalar field over angular coordinates, while a 3D globe is an embedding of a two-dimensional parameter domain into Euclidean space. The “projection onto a sphere” is exactly the passage from a scalar radius function \(r(\lambda,\varphi)\) to the embedded surface \(X(\lambda,\varphi)=r(\lambda,\varphi)\,\hat{\mathbf{u}}(\lambda,\varphi)\).

I will assume familiarity with multivariable calculus, differential geometry of surfaces, linear algebra, and numerical discretization. The note is written to be self-contained, so I will restate the needed constructions explicitly.

Conventions: longitude is denoted by \( \lambda \in [-\pi,\pi) \), latitude by \( \varphi \in [-\pi/2,\pi/2] \), and the mean lunar reference radius by \(R_0\). For LOLA products, \(R_0 = 1{,}737{,}400\ \text{m}\) is the standard global reference radius used in the GeoTIFF metadata we inspected.

1. What the LOLA Raster Actually Represents

A global LOLA DEM is not initially a surface in \(\mathbb{R}^3\). It is a sampled scalar field on a two-dimensional parameter domain. In the global simple cylindrical product, each raster cell corresponds to a pair of angular coordinates \((\lambda,\varphi)\), and the stored value represents elevation relative to a reference sphere.

Thus the fundamental continuous object is \[ h : (\lambda,\varphi) \mapsto h(\lambda,\varphi), \] where \(h\) is height above the reference sphere. The physical radius is then \[ r(\lambda,\varphi) = R_0 + h(\lambda,\varphi). \]

In the particular TIFF we used, the stored pixel values are signed 16-bit integers with scale factor \(0.5\). If \(q_{ij}\in\mathbb{Z}\) denotes the stored raster sample, then the physical elevation is \[ h_{ij} = 0.5\, q_{ij}\ \text{meters}, \] except for the designated nodata value \(q_{ij}=-32768\).

This matters because one must distinguish three different scalar fields:

  • The stored integer field \(q_{ij}\).
  • The metric elevation field \(h_{ij}\).
  • The metric radius field \(r_{ij}=R_0+h_{ij}\).

Confusing these fields produces nonsense. In particular, interpreting the TIFF bytes directly as generic intensities or as unsigned values destroys the geometry.

2. The Parameter Domain and the Cylindrical Grid

Let the raster width be \(W\) and height be \(H\). A global simple cylindrical raster defines a rectangular parameter domain \[ (\lambda,\varphi)\in[-\pi,\pi)\times[-\pi/2,\pi/2]. \] If one uses pixel centers, then the sample coordinates are \[ \lambda_j = -\pi + 2\pi\frac{j+\tfrac12}{W}, \qquad \varphi_i = \frac{\pi}{2} - \pi\frac{i+\tfrac12}{H}, \] for \(j=0,\dots,W-1\) and \(i=0,\dots,H-1\).

This is the first “projection” in the story: the sphere has been flattened into a rectangle. The flattening is not area-preserving. At fixed angular spacings \(\Delta\lambda,\Delta\varphi\), the corresponding physical east-west arc length on the sphere is \[ R_0\cos\varphi\,\Delta\lambda, \] while the north-south arc length is \[ R_0\,\Delta\varphi. \] Therefore the raster cells represent smaller and smaller physical areas as \(|\varphi|\to\pi/2\).

The area element on the reference sphere is \[ dA = R_0^2\cos\varphi\, d\lambda\, d\varphi. \] The factor \(\cos\varphi\) is the Jacobian determinant of the spherical parameterization. Any differential operator or averaging procedure that ignores this factor is biased toward polar regions.

3. From Angular Coordinates to Euclidean Coordinates

Define the unit radial direction \[ \hat{\mathbf{u}}(\lambda,\varphi) = \begin{pmatrix} \cos\varphi\cos\lambda \\ \sin\varphi \\ \cos\varphi\sin\lambda \end{pmatrix}. \] This is the standard latitude-longitude parameterization of the unit sphere \(S^2\subset\mathbb{R}^3\), written in a \(y\)-up coordinate convention suitable for most graphics pipelines.

The embedded lunar surface is then \[ X(\lambda,\varphi) = r(\lambda,\varphi)\,\hat{\mathbf{u}}(\lambda,\varphi). \] If \(r\equiv R_0\), this is exactly the reference sphere. If \(r\) varies, the surface is a radial graph over the sphere.

Explicitly, \[ X(\lambda,\varphi) = \begin{pmatrix} r(\lambda,\varphi)\cos\varphi\cos\lambda \\ r(\lambda,\varphi)\sin\varphi \\ r(\lambda,\varphi)\cos\varphi\sin\lambda \end{pmatrix}. \] This formula is the exact mathematical content of “projecting the LOLA data onto a sphere.”

A useful viewpoint is that the Moon here is not approximated by a sphere and then perturbed. It is modeled as a radial graph over a sphere. The reference sphere is the parameter base, not the final geometry.

4. Differential Geometry of the Embedded Surface

To understand shading, normals, metric distortion, and why equirectangular sampling behaves differently by latitude, one should compute the tangent vectors. Write \(r_\lambda=\partial r/\partial\lambda\) and \(r_\varphi=\partial r/\partial\varphi\). Then \[ X_\lambda = r_\lambda \hat{\mathbf{u}} + r\, \hat{\mathbf{u}}_\lambda, \qquad X_\varphi = r_\varphi \hat{\mathbf{u}} + r\, \hat{\mathbf{u}}_\varphi. \]

The relevant geometric object here is the first fundamental form of the embedded lunar surface. Since the map \[ X : (\lambda,\varphi) \mapsto X(\lambda,\varphi)\in\mathbb{R}^3 \] defines a parametrized surface in Euclidean space, the first fundamental form is the pullback of the Euclidean metric on \(\mathbb{R}^3\) along \(X\). In other words, if \(g_{\mathbb{R}^3}\) denotes the standard Euclidean inner product, then \[ I = X^* g_{\mathbb{R}^3}. \] This is the induced metric on the surface, written in the coordinates \((\lambda,\varphi)\). In coordinates \((\lambda,\varphi)\), that metric is \[ I = \begin{pmatrix} X_\lambda\cdot X_\lambda & X_\lambda\cdot X_\varphi \\ X_\varphi\cdot X_\lambda & X_\varphi\cdot X_\varphi \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} E & F \\ F & G \end{pmatrix}. \] To see where this matrix comes from, take an infinitesimal displacement in parameter space: \[ dX = X_\lambda\, d\lambda + X_\varphi\, d\varphi. \] The squared physical line element is then computed using the Euclidean dot product: \[ ds^2 = dX\cdot dX. \] Expanding gives \[ ds^2 = (X_\lambda\cdot X_\lambda)\, d\lambda^2 + 2(X_\lambda\cdot X_\varphi)\, d\lambda\, d\varphi + (X_\varphi\cdot X_\varphi)\, d\varphi^2. \] Hence \[ ds^2 = E\, d\lambda^2 + 2F\, d\lambda\, d\varphi + G\, d\varphi^2. \] So the coefficients \(E,F,G\) measure how longitude-latitude coordinate changes translate into actual metric distances on the embedded Moon.

Since \[ \hat{\mathbf{u}}_\lambda = \begin{pmatrix} -\cos\varphi\sin\lambda \\ 0 \\ \cos\varphi\cos\lambda \end{pmatrix}, \qquad \hat{\mathbf{u}}_\varphi = \begin{pmatrix} -\sin\varphi\cos\lambda \\ \cos\varphi \\ -\sin\varphi\sin\lambda \end{pmatrix}, \] we get a local basis for the tangent plane.

The first fundamental form has coefficients \[ E = X_\lambda\cdot X_\lambda, \qquad F = X_\lambda\cdot X_\varphi, \qquad G = X_\varphi\cdot X_\varphi. \] To simplify these expressions, note that \(\hat{\mathbf{u}}\) is a unit vector field, so \[ \hat{\mathbf{u}}\cdot \hat{\mathbf{u}} = 1. \] Differentiating with respect to \(\lambda\) gives \[ \frac{\partial}{\partial\lambda}(\hat{\mathbf{u}}\cdot\hat{\mathbf{u}}) = 2\,\hat{\mathbf{u}}\cdot\hat{\mathbf{u}}_\lambda = 0, \] hence \[ \hat{\mathbf{u}}\cdot\hat{\mathbf{u}}_\lambda = 0. \] Likewise, \[ \hat{\mathbf{u}}\cdot\hat{\mathbf{u}}_\varphi = 0. \] Geometrically, \(\hat{\mathbf{u}}\) is the radial unit normal to the reference sphere, while \(\hat{\mathbf{u}}_\lambda\) and \(\hat{\mathbf{u}}_\varphi\) are tangent to the sphere because they describe how the point on the unit sphere moves as longitude or latitude changes. Therefore these tangent derivatives are orthogonal to the radial direction. Using this, one finds \[ E = r_\lambda^2 + r^2\cos^2\varphi, \qquad F = r_\lambda r_\varphi, \qquad G = r_\varphi^2 + r^2. \]

In the special case \(r\equiv R_0\), these reduce to \[ E = R_0^2\cos^2\varphi,\qquad F=0,\qquad G=R_0^2, \] which is exactly the standard metric on the sphere in latitude-longitude coordinates.

The surface area element is \[ dA_{\text{surface}} = \|X_\lambda\times X_\varphi\|\, d\lambda\, d\varphi = \sqrt{EG-F^2}\, d\lambda\, d\varphi. \] This is the infinitesimal area of the parallelogram spanned by the tangent vectors \(X_\lambda\, d\lambda\) and \(X_\varphi\, d\varphi\). Equivalently, \((d\lambda,d\varphi)\) determines an infinitesimal rectangle in parameter space, and the factor \(\|X_\lambda\times X_\varphi\|\) is the Jacobian that converts parameter-space area into physical area on the embedded surface.

The identity \[ \|X_\lambda\times X_\varphi\|^2 = (X_\lambda\cdot X_\lambda)(X_\varphi\cdot X_\varphi) - (X_\lambda\cdot X_\varphi)^2 = EG-F^2 \] is the Gram determinant formula for the area of a parallelogram in Euclidean space. That is why the same area element can be written either as \(\|X_\lambda\times X_\varphi\|\, d\lambda\, d\varphi\) or as \(\sqrt{EG-F^2}\, d\lambda\, d\varphi\).

For small topography relative to \(R_0\), this is approximately \[ dA_{\text{surface}} \approx r^2\cos\varphi \sqrt{ 1 + \frac{r_\varphi^2}{r^2} + \frac{r_\lambda^2}{r^2\cos^2\varphi} }\, d\lambda\, d\varphi. \]

5. Normal Vector and Shading Geometry

A graphics engine needs a normal field. The exact surface normal is \[ \mathbf{n}(\lambda,\varphi) = \frac{X_\lambda\times X_\varphi}{\|X_\lambda\times X_\varphi\|}. \] For intuition, one can derive a first-order approximation when \(|h|\ll R_0\). In that regime the normal differs from the radial direction by the spherical gradient of the height field.

On the reference sphere of radius \(R_0\), the gradient of a scalar function \(h(\lambda,\varphi)\) is \[ \nabla_{S^2} h = \frac{1}{R_0\cos\varphi}\frac{\partial h}{\partial\lambda}\,\mathbf{e}_\lambda + \frac{1}{R_0}\frac{\partial h}{\partial\varphi}\,\mathbf{e}_\varphi, \] where \(\mathbf{e}_\lambda\) and \(\mathbf{e}_\varphi\) are the orthonormal east and north tangent vectors. It is worth deriving this explicitly. On the reference sphere, \[ ds^2 = R_0^2\cos^2\varphi\, d\lambda^2 + R_0^2\, d\varphi^2, \] so the coordinate basis vectors have lengths \[ \|X_\lambda\| = R_0\cos\varphi, \qquad \|X_\varphi\| = R_0. \] Hence the associated orthonormal tangent frame is \[ \mathbf{e}_\lambda = \frac{X_\lambda}{R_0\cos\varphi}, \qquad \mathbf{e}_\varphi = \frac{X_\varphi}{R_0}. \]

By definition, the gradient is the unique tangent vector field satisfying \[ \langle \nabla_{S^2} h, V\rangle = dh(V) \] for every tangent vector \(V\). Writing \[ \nabla_{S^2} h = a\,\mathbf{e}_\lambda + b\,\mathbf{e}_\varphi, \] and testing first against \(V=\mathbf{e}_\lambda\), we get \[ a = \langle \nabla_{S^2} h,\mathbf{e}_\lambda\rangle = dh(\mathbf{e}_\lambda) = \frac{1}{R_0\cos\varphi}\frac{\partial h}{\partial\lambda}. \] Likewise, testing against \(V=\mathbf{e}_\varphi\) yields \[ b = \frac{1}{R_0}\frac{\partial h}{\partial\varphi}. \] Therefore \[ \nabla_{S^2} h = \frac{1}{R_0\cos\varphi}\frac{\partial h}{\partial\lambda}\,\mathbf{e}_\lambda + \frac{1}{R_0}\frac{\partial h}{\partial\varphi}\,\mathbf{e}_\varphi. \] Consequently, \[ \mathbf{n} \approx \frac{ \hat{\mathbf{u}} - \nabla_{S^2}h }{ \|\hat{\mathbf{u}} - \nabla_{S^2}h\| }. \]

This formula explains the numerical hillshade recipe. If one estimates \(h_\lambda\) and \(h_\varphi\) by finite differences, rescales the longitudinal derivative by \(\cos\varphi\), and then combines it with a light direction \(\mathbf{s}\in S^2\), the shading intensity is approximately \[ I(\lambda,\varphi) = \max(0,\mathbf{n}\cdot\mathbf{s}). \]

The important geometric correction is the factor \(1/\cos\varphi\) in the east-west derivative. Without it, topography at high latitudes is artificially flattened or exaggerated depending on the convention.

6. Discrete Sampling and Texture Coordinates

A rendering engine does not use the continuous map \(X(\lambda,\varphi)\) directly. It samples it on a mesh. For a sphere geometry with \(N_\lambda\) longitudinal segments and \(N_\varphi\) latitudinal segments, one creates vertices \[ (\lambda_j,\varphi_i), \qquad \lambda_j = 2\pi\frac{j}{N_\lambda}, \qquad \varphi_i = \frac{\pi}{2} - \pi\frac{i}{N_\varphi}, \] then associates to each vertex a texture coordinate \[ u = \frac{\lambda + \pi}{2\pi},\qquad v = \frac{\frac{\pi}{2}-\varphi}{\pi}. \]

The displacement map is therefore a sampled version of the scalar field \(r\) or of a normalized affine transform of it. If the raster stores a normalized texture \[ d(\lambda,\varphi)\in[0,1], \] then one typically reconstructs metric radius by \[ r(\lambda,\varphi) = a\, d(\lambda,\varphi) + b, \] where \(a\) and \(b\) are chosen from the original data range. In our pipeline, \[ d = \frac{r-r_{\min}}{r_{\max}-r_{\min}}, \] so the inverse map is \[ r = r_{\min} + (r_{\max}-r_{\min})d. \]

In a three.js material, one usually works on a unit sphere and lets the displacement act in normalized units. If the geometric base radius is \(1\), then one sets \[ \text{displacementScale} = \frac{r_{\max}-r_{\min}}{R_0}, \qquad \text{displacementBias} = \frac{r_{\min}}{R_0} - 1. \] Indeed, for normalized displacement \(d\), \[ 1 + \text{displacementBias} + \text{displacementScale}\cdot d = \frac{r_{\min}}{R_0} + \frac{r_{\max}-r_{\min}}{R_0}d = \frac{r}{R_0}. \]

This equality is the precise reason those parameters reconstruct the physical radius field on a unit reference sphere.

7. Resampling the Raster

The raw LOLA raster is much higher resolution than a practical WebGL mesh or texture. Therefore one chooses a reduced grid \[ W' \times H', \] with \(H'=W'/2\) for a global equirectangular texture. Mathematically this is the problem of approximating \(r(\lambda,\varphi)\) by a lower-resolution sampled field.

Let \(r_{ij}\) be the original discrete samples on the fine grid and \(r'_{ab}\) the coarse samples. A nearest-neighbor scheme chooses \[ r'_{ab} = r_{i(a),j(b)}, \] where \(i(a),j(b)\) map coarse indices to the nearest fine indices. Bilinear interpolation instead approximates \(r\) locally by an affine function on each raster cell and samples that interpolant.

From a numerical analysis viewpoint, nearest-neighbor preserves observed values but aliases high-frequency topography. Bilinear interpolation is smoother but acts as a low-pass filter. More sophisticated schemes, such as windowed sinc or area-weighted spherical averaging, better preserve physical meaning, especially if one cares about integrated quantities.

There is another subtlety: because the source grid is equi-angular rather than equal-area, naive averaging of pixel values is not geometrically neutral. If one wants a coarse value representing an average over a latitude band, one should weight by \(\cos\varphi\). For example, a physically meaningful average of a field \(f\) over a region \(\Omega\) is \[ \frac{\int_\Omega f(\lambda,\varphi)\cos\varphi\, d\lambda\, d\varphi} {\int_\Omega \cos\varphi\, d\lambda\, d\varphi}. \]

8. Why the Poles Are Numerically Awkward

Latitude-longitude coordinates are singular at the poles. The map from the rectangle to the sphere identifies every longitude at \(\varphi=\pm\pi/2\). Therefore:

  • Longitudinal derivatives become ill-conditioned as \(\cos\varphi\to 0\).
  • Raster cells collapse in physical width near the poles.
  • Triangulated sphere meshes accumulate many narrow triangles there.

The singularity is a coordinate artifact, not a geometric singularity of the Moon. The surface \(X\) remains regular provided \(r\) is regular. But finite-difference formulas written in \((\lambda,\varphi)\) coordinates must account for the \(1/\cos\varphi\) factor or be regularized near the poles.

In practice one clips \(\cos\varphi\) away from zero when producing hillshade from an equirectangular raster. That is a numerical stabilization, not a deep geometric correction.

9. Relation to Ellipsoids and Why a Sphere Is Acceptable Here

For Earth, geodetic versus geocentric latitude and ellipsoidal reference surfaces matter substantially. For the Moon, many global products are distributed relative to a reference sphere rather than a flattened ellipsoid. This means the radial-graph model above is not just a convenient approximation; it matches the semantics of the dataset.

If one instead had heights above an ellipsoid, the analogous embedding would be more complicated. One would first map angular coordinates to the ellipsoid, then displace along the appropriate normal or radial direction according to the dataset definition. The present note is simpler because LOLA’s global simple cylindrical product is naturally interpreted as a radius perturbation of a sphere.

10. The End-to-End Mathematical Pipeline

The full construction can now be summarized cleanly.

  1. Decode each TIFF sample \(q_{ij}\) as signed int16 and convert to metric elevation \[ h_{ij} = s\, q_{ij} + c, \] with scale \(s=0.5\) m and offset \(c=0\).
  2. Form physical radii \[ r_{ij} = R_0 + h_{ij}. \]
  3. Associate each sample with angular coordinates \((\lambda_j,\varphi_i)\) on the global cylindrical grid.
  4. Resample \(r\) onto a practical output grid \(W'\times H'\).
  5. Normalize the resampled radii to a texture field \(d\in[0,1]\) for the renderer.
  6. Recover the embedded surface in the renderer by the formula \[ X(\lambda,\varphi) = r(\lambda,\varphi)\,\hat{\mathbf{u}}(\lambda,\varphi). \]
  7. Compute or approximate normals from the derivatives of the radius field if one wants visually meaningful terrain shading.

Every step has a precise mathematical meaning. The raster is a sampled scalar field. The spherical globe is an embedding. The displacement map is an affine encoding of the radius function. The lighting derives from the induced surface normal.

11. What Is Lost in the Visualization

A final caution is worth making. The rendered globe is not the same object as the original measurement archive. Several approximations intervene:

  • The continuous field is replaced by a raster sample set.
  • The raw raster is downsampled to a coarser texture.
  • The sphere mesh has finite tessellation, so displacement is only vertexwise.
  • Shading often uses approximate normals or bump maps rather than exact geometric derivatives.
  • Vertical exaggeration may be applied for visual legibility.

None of this invalidates the construction. It just means one should distinguish carefully between the geometric model, the sampled representation of the model, and the rendered approximation of that sampled representation.

12. Closing View

The mathematical heart of the problem is compact: a lunar DEM is a scalar field on angular coordinates, and a 3D Moon is the radial graph of that scalar field over the unit sphere. Everything else in the pipeline is bookkeeping for discretization, normalization, and numerically stable approximation of derivatives.

If one wants to be maximally formal, the constructed Moon is the image of the map \[ X : [-\pi,\pi)\times[-\pi/2,\pi/2] \to \mathbb{R}^3, \qquad X(\lambda,\varphi)=\bigl(R_0+h(\lambda,\varphi)\bigr)\hat{\mathbf{u}}(\lambda,\varphi), \] with periodic identification in longitude and coordinate singularities at the poles. That is the precise object your renderer is approximating.
