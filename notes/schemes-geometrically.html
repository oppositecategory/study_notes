Algebraic Geometry

Schemes, Geometrically

A guided return to schemes for readers who know some algebra, remember the classical story, and want the deeper mathematical reason the subject had to move beyond varieties.

Classical algebraic geometry often begins with zero sets of polynomials over an algebraically closed field. That viewpoint is valuable, but it is too narrow for modern algebraic geometry. It suppresses nilpotents, handles arithmetic badly, and does not express local-to-global constructions in their natural form. Scheme theory fixes this by assigning geometry directly to commutative rings.

The basic object of scheme theory is not merely a set of solutions. It is a space equipped with a sheaf of rings, so that the geometry remembers local functions, infinitesimal structure, and arithmetic information.

The goal of this note is to explain, in a self-contained way, why the construction A↦Spec(A) is the correct bridge from commutative algebra to geometry. I will proceed in stages: first the algebra, then the topological space, then the structure sheaf, and finally the passage from affine schemes to general schemes.

Standing convention: all rings are commutative and have a multiplicative identity.

1. Algebraic Preliminaries

A ring A is a set equipped with addition and multiplication satisfying the usual axioms. An ideal I of A is an additive subgroup such that ax∈I whenever a∈A and x∈I.

Given an ideal I, the quotient ring A/I is obtained by declaring every element of I to be zero. Concretely, two elements of A become equal in the quotient exactly when their difference lies in I.

An element a∈A is called nilpotent if an=0 for some integer n>0. A ring is called reduced if it has no nonzero nilpotent elements.

An ideal p is prime if p≠A and whenever ab∈p, then a∈p or b∈p. Equivalently, p is prime exactly when the quotient A/p is an integral domain.

2. The First Examples of Quotients

The distinction between the quotients k[x](x) and k[x](x2) is the standard first sign that classical point-set geometry throws away information schemes will keep.

In the quotient k[x](x), the element x becomes zero. Therefore every polynomial a0+a1x+a2x2+⋯ collapses to its constant term a0. Hence k[x](x)≅k.

In the quotient k[x](x2), only x2 is forced to vanish. The class of x is still nonzero, but its square is zero. Thus every element has the form a+bx, and x is a nonzero nilpotent.

As sets of k-valued solutions, both quotients correspond to the single equation x=0. But as rings they are different, and the second ring carries infinitesimal information that the first does not. Scheme theory will keep that distinction.

The two quotients have the same ordinary point but different ring structure. Schemes detect that difference.

3. Definition of Spec(A)

Let A be a commutative ring. The spectrum of A, denoted Spec(A), is first defined as the set of all prime ideals of A.

This already explains why fields give one-point spectra: if A is a field, its only prime ideal is (0). By contrast, for A=Z, the prime ideals are (0) and the ideals (p) for prime numbers p.

Why prime ideals rather than maximal ideals? Because prime ideals are exactly the ideals whose quotients still behave like single irreducible algebraic pieces. Maximal ideals are too few: they capture closed points, but not the generic points of irreducible subspaces.

For example, in k[x,y]:

  • (x-a,y-b) is maximal and corresponds to the closed point (a,b).
  • (y-x2) is prime and corresponds to the entire parabola as one irreducible subspace.
  • (0) is prime because the polynomial ring is a domain; it corresponds to the generic point of the whole affine plane.

4. The Zariski Topology

A set of prime ideals is not yet enough. To make Spec(A) into a geometric object, one equips it with a topology. For an ideal I of A, define

V(I) = { p∈Spec(A) :I⊆p }

The sets V(I) are declared to be the closed sets. This is the Zariski topology. It is designed so that closed sets record common vanishing conditions.

The basic open sets are complements of principal closed sets:

D(f) = Spec(A)\V(f) = {p:f∉p}

These are the points where f does not vanish. The topology is coarse compared with Euclidean topology, but it is exactly the topology that interacts correctly with commutative algebra.

Closure, Specialization, and Generic Points

If p∈Spec(A), then the closure of the singleton {p} is

{p} ¯ = V(p)

A point q is called a specialization of p if q∈{p}¯. Equivalently, this means p⊆q.

A point is closed if its singleton is closed. A point η is called a generic point of an irreducible closed subset Z if {η}¯=Z. In spectra, every irreducible closed subset has a unique generic point.

Generic points and specialization are not informal pictures; they come directly from the closure relation in the Zariski topology.
Summary: closed points correspond to maximal ideals, generic points correspond to irreducible closed subsets, and specialization is just inclusion of prime ideals.

5. Why a Topological Space Alone Is Not Enough

Up to this point Spec(A) is only a topological space. But geometry is not just about points and closed sets; it is also about functions. To recover the correct local algebra, one equips the spectrum with a structure sheaf.

The key algebraic construction here is localization. If quotienting means "force some elements to be zero," localization means "force some elements to become invertible."

For a basic open set D(f), the ring of regular functions is Af, the localization of A obtained by inverting powers of f.

Concretely, if A=k[x], then in Ax one may form 1x, x+1x3, and similar fractions, because on the open set where x does not vanish such denominators are legitimate.

The sheaf is defined so that these localizations are precisely the regular functions on basic open sets. Thus an affine scheme is not just the topological space Spec(A), but the pair

(Spec(A),OSpec(A))

where OSpec(A) is the structure sheaf.

Local Rings and Residue Fields

For a point p∈Spec(A), the stalk of the structure sheaf is the local ring

OSpec(A),p = Ap

This ring is obtained by inverting every element of A that is not in p. Intuitively, it is the ring of functions defined near the point p.

The residue field at p is

k(p) = Ap pAp

It records the values of regular functions at the point. For a closed k-rational point over an algebraically closed field this is often just k, but in general it can be a larger field.

Local rings are not auxiliary gadgets; they are part of the definition of scheme-theoretic geometry.

6. Affine Schemes and Morphisms

An affine scheme is a locally ringed space isomorphic to Spec(A) for some ring A. Affine schemes are the local models for all schemes.

The fundamental algebra-geometry dictionary is contravariant:

Hom(A,B) ≅ Hom(Spec(B),Spec(A))

A ring map A→B induces a map of spectra by sending a prime q⊆B to its inverse image f-1(q)⊆A. The direction reverses because functions pull back.

This is one of the main reasons schemes are powerful: many geometric constructions can be reduced to ring-theoretic ones and then translated back.

7. From Affine Schemes to General Schemes

Most natural objects are not affine. The solution is to glue affine schemes along open subsets, exactly as one builds manifolds by gluing Euclidean charts.

Formally, a scheme is a locally ringed space X such that every point of X has an open neighborhood isomorphic to an affine scheme.

A scheme is locally affine. The global object is obtained by gluing local spectra compatibly.

The standard example is projective line Pk1, which is covered by two affine lines glued along the open sets where the coordinates are nonzero.

Pk1 = U0∪U1 U0≅Spec(k[t]), U1≅Spec(k[s]) on the overlap,s=1t

8. Why Schemes Are Strictly More General Than Varieties

The scheme-theoretic framework enlarges classical algebraic geometry in several precise ways.

  • It allows arbitrary commutative rings, not just finitely generated reduced algebras over a field.
  • It keeps nilpotent structure, so infinitesimal data is visible.
  • It treats arithmetic objects such as Spec(Z) on the same footing as geometric ones.
  • It is local by construction, so gluing and localization become fundamental rather than ad hoc.

In particular, adjectives such as reduced, irreducible, and integral have exact ring-theoretic meanings:

  • Spec(A) is reduced if and only if A is reduced.
  • Spec(A) is irreducible if and only if the nilradical of A is prime.
  • Spec(A) is integral if and only if A is an integral domain.

These are not slogans; they are precise equivalences. This is why affine scheme geometry is often described as commutative algebra made geometric.

9. Closing Summary

The construction A↦Spec(A) proceeds in layers:

  • Take prime ideals as points.
  • Use the Zariski topology, whose closed sets are the sets V(I).
  • Add the structure sheaf so that functions on D(f) are elements of Af.
  • Recover local behavior through local rings Ap.
  • Glue affine schemes to obtain general schemes.

Once all of these ingredients are present, schemes stop looking like an artificial complication. They are simply the correct level of generality for doing geometry in a way that is faithful to commutative algebra.
